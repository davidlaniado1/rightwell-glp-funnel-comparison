3. INDIVIDUAL FUNNEL ANALYSIS

RemedyMeds — 8.5/10

Wants the customer to believe: Obesity is biology (food noise, hormones, weight cycling), not willpower — so medication plus clinicians is the logical fix, and the only risk is not starting (warranty). Why the hook works: It reframes blame in the first sentence. Every competitor sells a discount; Remedy sells a belief. The $120-off banner rides on top without being the message. What creates desire: The pace selector (you choose 3/6/12 months) and the personalized projection ("Lose 32 lbs by December 11") make the result feel scheduled, not hoped for. What creates trust: JAMA citation, outcome stats with methodology footnotes, Trustpilot 4.7 pinned on every slide, clinician headshot, real care phone number in the header, "why we ask" microcopy. What creates doubt: $299/mo flat recurring lands only at checkout; microdose/name-brand lines add choice overload on the lander. Where it loses momentum: The approved page is long — congrats hero to plan cards is a lot of scrolling before price. Best section: Quiz (16 screens, every one earns its place). Weakest section: Lander product-line pickers (4 options before the quiz is premature). Best copy idea: "If it doesn't work, you don't pay." Best design idea: Warranty seal ON the Complete Purchase button. Best offer idea: Refunded if not prescribed. RightWell should copy: Approval-before-contact-capture sequencing; why-we-ask microcopy; warranty on the pay button. RightWell should not copy: Flat monthly pricing — our plan ladder out-monetizes it. Priority recommendation: This is the funnel to model for slide quality.

TrimRx — 7.5/10

Wants the customer to believe: This is the trusted, press-vetted GLP-1 brand, you have nothing to risk because you pay nothing today. Why the hook works/fails: The pre-lander ("What is your weight loss goal?") is a serviceable click-commitment gate but installs no idea. The funnel's real hook is at checkout: $0 due today. What creates desire: Three before/after photo testimonials timed between question blocks; personalized "Losing 32lbs is easier than you think." What creates trust: Forbes/WebMD/Yahoo masthead on every screen; Yahoo "Best Overall 9.8" interstitial; the medical depth itself (BP, heart rate) reads clinical. What creates doubt: "Only 31 discounts left… reserved for 9:38" and "12,256 chose this today" smell manufactured to anyone paying attention. Where it loses momentum: Screens 20–35. Nine yes/no medical screens in a row with no break is where dropoff lives. Best section: Checkout step 3 ($0 due today + code applied). Weakest section: The mid-quiz medical slog. Best copy idea: "$0 Due Today — only charged if your prescription is approved." Best design idea: Persistent press masthead. Best offer idea: First-month anchor $299→$159. RightWell should copy: $0-due-today framing; persistent trust header; photo testimonials in-quiz. RightWell should not copy: 36-screen length; fabricated discount-inventory scarcity. Priority recommendation: Steal the charge-on-approval mechanic before anyone else in our lane does.

DirectMeds — 6.5/10

Wants the customer to believe: Same medication as the big brands, $147, delivered in 2 days — a price-and-speed play. Why the hook works/fails: "$147" with a weight-loss calculator is a strong cold-traffic price hook. But the funnel is a word-for-word TrimRx template clone, executed with less polish and more aggression. What creates desire: The interactive "you could easily lose 44 lbs" slider; cheapest entry of the four. What creates trust: 200k customers claim, FAQ depth, in-house nursing-staff claim. What creates doubt: "SARAH'S APPROVAL IS VALID FOR 29:56" — approval that expires in half an hour reads fake because it is. The $147→$297/mo jump is in the order summary fine print. Where it loses momentum: Nowhere structurally (it's the proven template) — it leaks at credibility, not flow. Best section: The $49.95 "Skip the Line" order bump — the single smartest monetization idea across all four funnels. Weakest section: The 30-minute approval countdown. Best copy idea: "Your medication price never goes up — even when your dose increases." Best design idea: Recommendation block ("we recommend Semaglutide Weekly Injections… but you can choose"). Best offer idea: Skip-the-line bump. RightWell should copy: The bump; the personalized recommendation block. RightWell should not copy: Expiring-approval urgency; "easily lose 44 lbs"; entry-price bait with a hidden treadmill. Priority recommendation: Take the bump, leave everything else.

RightWell (ours) — 7.0/10

Wants the customer to believe: Most trusted GLP-1 provider, big savings today, approved fast. Why the hook works/fails: The /get-started lander is efficient — promo banner, award badges, goal question above the fold. But "most trusted provider" is a category claim, not an idea. There's no belief being installed about the buyer's problem. What creates desire: $100 off + free ToneProtect gift; weight-projection graph on the approved page. What creates trust: Lander badges (Best of 2026, Trustpilot 4.7) — then trust goes silent for 17 quiz screens. The 92.8% "Medical Review" score is good gamification. What creates doubt: "Voted most trusted GLP-1 provider" sourced to TopIQ/Consumer Radar will not survive a skeptical buyer's google. "You are approved!" before any clinician saw anything. Where it loses momentum: The quiz interior. Logo-only header, no images, no social proof, no why-we-ask — 17 screens of blank corridor between a strong lander and a strong checkout. Best section: Checkout (gift + guarantee + plan ladder + Klarna + Apple Pay is the most complete offer stack of the four). Weakest section: Every quiz slide between entry and medical review. Priority recommendation: Fix slide quality first — it's the cheapest fix with the most surface area.

6. WHAT TO REMOVE OR AVOID

  • Fabricated inventory scarcity ("Only 31 discounts left" — TrimRx). Weak because it's checkably false; trains buyers to distrust the whole page. Compliance: deceptive-practice exposure (FTC).
  • Expiring approval ("valid for 29:56" — DirectMeds). Medical approval that lapses in 30 minutes reads fake and cheapens the clinical story. Compliance: misleading urgency.
  • Live "chose this today" counters (TrimRx). Unverifiable, increasingly recognized as fake. Same FTC exposure.
  • Entry-price bait with flat treadmill (DM $147→$297). Converts the click, then manufactures month-2 chargebacks and refunds. We monetize with a visible ladder instead — keep it.
  • 36-screen intake (TrimRx). Sunk cost works until fatigue beats it; we win on speed — don't trade it away.

8. PRIORITY ACTION PLAN

Priority 1 — "$0 due today" charge-on-approval at checkout. What to change: authorize the card at checkout, charge only after provider approval; say so in the order summary. Why: removes the single biggest fear at the single highest-stakes moment; TrimRx proves it's operationally doable. Where: our payment section. Expected impact: highest of any single test (card-entry → purchase rate). Difficulty: Medium (billing logic). Risk: Low.

Priority 2 — Quiz slide upgrade: trust strip + images + why-we-ask. What to change: persistent Trustpilot/award header, clinician photo, one-line "why we ask" on medical screens, 1–2 image interstitials. Why: 17 of our screens currently do zero persuasion; every competitor works every slide. Where: all quiz screens. Expected impact: quiz completion rate, especially mid-funnel. Difficulty: Low (Heyflow edits). Risk: Low.

Priority 3 — First-month price test $149–159. What to change: monthly plan card anchor $299→$149-159 (vs current $199). Why: we're the most expensive entry of four funnels selling the same molecule; price shoppers never reach our (superior) plan ladder. Where: monthly plan card + surface "from $159/mo" on the lander. Expected impact: high on cold-traffic CVR; protects LTV via existing ladder. Difficulty: Low. Risk: Low (margin math first).

Priority 4 — $49.95 Priority Review bump. What to change: optional "priority provider review" toggle below card fields. Why: found money from already-committed buyers; nobody else in our funnel does it except DM. Where: checkout, below card fields. Expected impact: +AOV on every order that takes it, no CVR cost if optional. Difficulty: Low–Medium (ops must actually deliver fast review). Risk: Low.

Priority 5 — Reframe "You are approved!" + guarantee on the pay button. What to change: "Pre-qualified — a licensed provider reviews before you're charged" + "Backed by the RightWell Guarantee 🔒" on the Continue button. Why: kills our biggest compliance exposure and adds risk-reversal at the moment of fear in one edit. Where: checkout hero + Continue button. Expected impact: neutral-to-positive CVR, large risk reduction. Difficulty: Low. Risk: Reduces risk.

9. FINAL CEO DECISION

Best funnel to model: RemedyMeds — for slide quality and checkout bridge, not pricing. Best section to copy: TrimRx checkout step 3 ($0 due today + code-applied summary). Biggest RightWell weakness: An empty quiz — shortest path, thinnest persuasion. Fastest fix: Trust strip + why-we-ask microcopy on every quiz slide (days, not weeks). Highest-impact test: Charge-on-approval billing. Biggest compliance concern: Unconditional "You are approved!" before provider review, plus the lbs-specific guarantee. Do not do this: Import fake scarcity (discount inventory, expiring approvals, live counters) or copy DirectMeds' bait-entry pricing. Our plan ladder is the honest version of the same economics — keep it.
